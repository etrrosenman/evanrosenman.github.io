Senior Thesis

"Retweets—but Not Just Retweets: Quantifying and Predicting Influence on Twitter" [PDF]

  1. Evan Rosenman, March 2012. Submitted in partial fulfillment of the honors requirements for a degree in Applied Mathematics.

  2. Abstract:
    There has recently been a sharp uptick in interest among researchers and private firms in determining how to quantify influence on the microblogging site Twitter. We restrict our attention solely to celebrities, and using data collected from Twitter APIs in February and March 2012, we explore four different influence metrics for a group of 60 prominent and well-followed individuals. We find that retweet-based influence is the most significant type of influence, but other effects — like the adoption of hashtags and links — are comparable in terms of generated impressions, and are governed by fundamentally different dynamics. We use the insights from our analysis to develop predictive models of retweets, hashtag and link adoptions, and increases in follower counts. We find that, across different types of influence, the degree to which a celebrity is discussed on Twitter is an extremely useful predictor, while follower counts are comparatively less predictive.


Other Research Work

"A Mathematical Model of the Twitter Graph Structure" [PDF unavailable]

  1. Evan Rosenman, December 2011. Final project for Applied Math 115.

  2. Description:
    In this research paper, I sought to model the evolution of the Twitter follower graph by assigning each node (Twitter user) an "affinity" score, representing the user's intrinsic attractiveness to other users. I aimed to construct a graph which would resemble Twitter in both the skewed shape of its follower distribution and the high level of asymmetry in its following relationships. Ultimately, I found that the follower distribution was well-modelled by assigning user affinity scores according to a Chi-Squared Distribution and determining the probability that any node x follows another node y using both the absolute difference in the nodes' affinity scores and the number of inlinks to node y.

"Impact of Lithium-coated PFCs on the Edge Neutral Density on NSTX" [PDF unavailable]

  1. Evan Rosenman and Devon Battaglia, August 2010. Submitted upon completion of research project as a laboratory intern at Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory.

  2. Abstract:
    Wall recycling of deuterium is reduced on NSTX by applying solid lithium coatings to the carbon composite plasma-facing components. The impact of the reduced recycling on the neutral density profile in the scrape-off layer (SOL) is inferred using a high-speed camera (268 Hz) with an H-beta filter and a chordal view of the SOL (0.2 cm resolution). The recorded intensity profile is converted to a radial profile of plasma emissivity using an absolute calibration ofthe camera and an Abel Inversion. The neutral density is computedby dividing the plasma emissivity by a function of the electron density and temperature, which are estimated using data from the Thomson scattering diagnostic. Initial calculations indicate that the neutral deuterium density decreases as the total amount of pre-shot lithium deposited in NSTX increases. The error in the profile measurement is quantified via Monte Carlo techniques. This work is supported by US DOE contracts DE-AC02-09CH11466 and DE- AC05-00OR22725.


Writing Samples

"Imagining, Properly: Use and Misuse of the Fictive Mind in Sense and Sensibility" [PDF unavailable]

  1. Evan Rosenman, May 2012. Final paper for English 141: The 18th Century Novel.

  2. Description:
    Though critics have often focused on the titular dichotomy in Jane Austen's Sense and Sensibility, Austen offers a subtler dichotomy, and a more decisive commentary, in her presentation of the imaginative minds of the novel's two protagonists. Austen critiques Marianne Dashwood's overactive imagination — which causes her to become dangerously dissociated from reality — and Elinor Dashwood's hardened opposition to fantasy — which prevents her from picturing a positive future. In demonstrating the problems of each sister's perspective and depicting a reformation of imagination in each woman, Austen presents her belief that, when properly grounded, the fictive mind can be a useful tool for envisioning and working towards a better life.

"Emasculation of Racial Border-Crossers in Early Films of Spike Lee" [PDF unavailable]

  1. Evan Rosenman, December 2010. Final paper for African and African American Studies 121: Please Wake Up! Race, Gender and Ethnicity in The Early Films of Spike Lee.

  2. Description:
    In several of his early films, influential director Spike Lee presents racial border-crossing as an act of self-alienation, in which an individual becomes dislocated from his own social identity. In 1989's Do the Right Thing and 1991's Jungle Fever, Lee presents the transgression of racial boundaries as an undertaking that compromises a man's sense of power and masculinity. In these films, three male characters — Mookie, Flipper, and Paulie — establish relationships with a member of another race, and as a consequence they suffer a severe loss of agency in their own endeavors. In order to reclaim their sense of virility and control over their surroundings, each of these men ultimately makes another individual feel powerless. Lee offers a perilous vision of interracial interaction in which individuals can ultimately be driven to cruelty and aggression as a result of the dislocating effect of traversing racial boundaries.

"A Passing Phase or a Sexual Preference?
Understanding Homosexual Identities in Two Victorian Horror Stories" [PDF unavailable]

  1. Evan Rosenman, December 2009. Final paper for English 156: Crime and Horror in Victorian Literature and Culture.

  2. Description:
    While some Victorian horror writers embraced the prevailing views of the era, which condoned youthful homoeroticism as a developmental step toward heterosexual adulthood, others subtly argued for the acknowledgement of homosexuality as an alternative sexual identity. Such contrasting views can be found in two famous Victorian horror stories — Lady Audley's Secret, by Mary Elizabeth Braddon, and Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde, by Robert Louis Stevenson. Braddon strongly implies a homoerotic attachment between Robert Audley and his friend George, but she characterizes Robert's homosexual urges as an element of his youth that he must overcome in order to enter adulthood. In contrast, Stevenson's novella implies that the homosexuality of Dr. Jekyll is an indelible element of his character, and the doctor's destruction is presented as a consequence of his inability to reconcile his sexuality with social convention.

"Defining the Mother in the Modern World:
An Analysis of Surrogacy-Related Case Law and Its Implications" [PDF unavailable]

  1. Evan Rosenman, May 2010. Final paper for English 90xa: Crime and Law — Drama, Film, and Performance.

  2. Description:
    In recent years, the rise of surrogacy — the assisted reproductive practice in which a woman carries a child to term with the intent of surrendering that child to another individual or couple at the time of birth — has raised a number of legal questions about defining both maternity and paternity. Surrogacy can create up to three separate definitions of parents: the gamete providers who serve as the child's genetic ancestors, the surrogate mother who gestates the child, and the "commissioning" parents, who set in motion the child's creation. These complexities of legal identity are compounded by the fact that statutes and case law vary widely from state to state. Restricting our analysis to two states with ample case law, we we find that California case law has tended toward defining motherhood by a woman's intention to parent, while the prevailing legal definition in New Jersey characterizes motherhood by biological means. We analyze these conflicting definitions in parallel with the legal meaning of paternity, which has largely been defined by intentionality, rather than genetics. Lastly, we address the ways in which this changing definition of parenthood could have enormous implications for the ways in which we define the social construct of the family.
